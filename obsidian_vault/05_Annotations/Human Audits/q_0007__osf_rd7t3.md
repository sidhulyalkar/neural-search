## Dataset
**ExPra 2026- Effects of Irrelevant Speech and Office Noise on Working Memory Performance in an N-Back Task**

Previous research shows that irrelevant speech can impair working memory performance (e.g., Banbury &amp; Berry, 1998; Jones &amp; Macken, 1993; Neath, 2000; Salamé &amp; Baddeley, 1982). This so-called irrelevant speech or unattended speech effect describes the impairment of memory performance caused by linguistic background stimuli that are irrelevant to the task. One explanation for this effect is that the irrelevant speech interferes with the information in the phonological memory (Jones &amp; Macken, 1993; Salamé &amp; Baddeley, 1982, 1989). Studies also show that the extent of the impairment is influenced by the phonological similarity of the element to be memorized (Salamé &amp; Baddeley, 1982), while the intensity of the irrelevant speech, at least for visually presented elements, has only a minor influence (Colle, 1980; Neath, 2000). This effect has been demonstrated in numerous studies, particularly in serial recall tasks, in which participants are asked to reproduce, for example, digits or letters in the exact same order after they have been presented (e.g., Campbell et al., 2002; Colle &amp; Welsh, 1976; Salamé &amp; Baddeley, 1982; Sörqvist, 2010). Other distracting noises, such as non-verbal office noise, have also been shown in studies to impair working memory tasks (Banbury &amp; Berry, 1998). However, few studies have examined the n-back task in the context of irrelevant speech and non-verbal background noise, even though it has become one of the most widely used approaches for studying working memory (Huang et al., 2025). First introduced by Kirchner (1958), this paradigm examines updating processes, maintenance, and attentional control by having participants decide whether the current item matches the stimulus presented n steps earlier. The aim of this study is therefore to investigate whether irrelevant speech or office noise also leads to impairments in an n-back task.
