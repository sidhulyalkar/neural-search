## Dataset
**Hippocampal subfields in adolescent early-onset psychosis: Sex differences and comparisons with adult psychosis**

Introduction
Sex differences in schizophrenia are evident in both clinical presentation and treatment outcomes. Schizophrenia is more prevalent in males, with a male-to-female ratio of 1.4:1 (Sommer et al., 2020), and males tend to have earlier psychosis onset (Selvendra et al., 2022), greater treatment resistance, and more severe negative symptoms compared to females (Ayesa-Arriola et al., 2020). However, the relationship between these clinical differences and brain morphology remains unclear. Exploring the connection between sex differences in clinical features and brain structure might yield insights into how schizophrenia manifests differently in males and females and can potentially facilitate the development of more tailored and effective treatments based on sex.
The hippocampus, a subcortical brain structure involved in learning, memory, and spatial navigation, has been shown to exhibit sex-related differences. Research indicates that males tend to have larger hippocampal volumes compared to females adjusted for brain size (van Eijk et al., 2020). Notably, lower hippocampal volume is a common morphological alteration observed in various mental health conditions, including psychotic disorders. These findings were first reported in postmortem studies (Roeske et al., 2021) and have later been replicated in structural brain imaging studies (Haukvik et al., 2018; van Erp et al., 2016). 
The hippocampus is comprised of cytoarchitectonically distinct subfields with differences in their anatomical connectivity and functional correlates, which suggests looking beyond total hippocampal volume. Sex differences have been found at hippocampal subfield level, revealing that males exhibit greater volumes in the fimbria and parasubiculum compared to females, while adjusting for total hippocampal volume. A meta-analysis of neuroimaging studies in schizophrenia and bipolar disorders found lower volumes in all hippocampal subfields (Haukvik et al., 2018). A recent study on sex differences in schizophrenia and bipolar disorder conducted by Barth et al (2024), showed higher volumes in healthy males relative to healthy females in the majority of hippocampal subfields, but no such sex differences or even reversed sex differences in the clinical groups. These findings suggest altered sex differences in hippocampal subfield volumes in psychotic disorders, particularly in schizophrenia, due to higher volumetric deficits in males. 
Although lower hippocampal subfield volumes are a well-established phenotype in adults with psychotic disorders, we know very little about brain morphological alterations in adolescents with early-onset psychosis (EOP) and how these relate to sex. EOP is defined as the emergence of psychosis during adolescence, i.e., before 19 years of age. Compared to adult-onset psychosis (AOP), EOP patients tend to have a higher prevalence of negative symptomology, more severe cognitive impairment (Salazar de Pablo et al., 2022), and worse long-term outcomes (Clemmensen et al., 2012). Similarly to adults with psychotic disorders, a significantly lower total hippocampus volume was also seen in EOP, with more prominent alteration in left hemisphere (Gurholt et al., 2020). The study did not find sex differences across healthy controls and EOP groups in terms of total hippocampus volume (Gurholt et al., 2020). However, volumetric diagnostic group and sex differences may not be detectible for whole hippocampus volumes, and studies (van Eijk et al., 2020) indicate sex differences on the level of specific subfields. The only study to-date investigating volumetric differences in hippocampal subfields in EOP compared to adolescent healthy controls found volume reductions of CA1, molecular layer, subiculum, para-subiculum, dentate gyrus and CA4 (Wang et al (2023)). The study did not examine sex-differences in hippocampal subfields in EOP. There have been no studies to date examining sex differences in hippocampal subfields in EOP. The main aim of the current study is to assess whether sex differences found in adult schizophrenia (Barth et al. (2024)) replicate in EOP. Understanding sex differences in hippocampal subfields in EOP can provide valuable insights into the neurobiological underpinnings of EOP and inform development of more targeted, sex-specific, interventions for affected adolescents.
The only study to-date investigating volumetric differences in hippocampal subfields in EOP compared to adolescent healthy controls found volume reductions of CA1, molecular layer, subiculum, para-subiculum, dentate gyrus and CA4 (Wang et al (2023)). Given that only one study has assessed hippocampal subfield differences in EOP patients relative to healthy controls, further research is essential, and it may be valuable to compare these findings with those from adult schizophrenia cohorts to elucidate potential differences in morphological alterations across disorders.
In this study, I will explore case-control differences and sex differences in hippocampal subfield volumes in patients with EOP compared to healthy controls. I will further investigate sex differences in the associations of hippocampal subfield volumes with medication use and symptomology. Furthermore, I will compare case-control and sex differences in hippocampal subfield volumes in the EOP sample to findings from an adult schizophrenia sample. Given there is no previous evidence on sex differences in hippocampal subfields in EOP, this study's analyses will be exploratory in nature.
